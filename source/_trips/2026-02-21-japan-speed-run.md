## Arrive

On the flight from Seattle, the guy next to me ended up being from SLC and we lamented the ski season we were having. Upon landing at Narita, I tried to my eSim working and it ended up being more complicated of a process than "it will just start working" than I expected, although me flailing in the settings probably wasn't helping.

The first challenge would be at the Yamato counter, but it went relatively smooth and I even ended up helping a couple figure out shipping their skis to the Harifu Welcome Center in Niseko. Getting the Welcome Suica card was the next challenge and that went the smoothest, but the area was bustling with people and made everything feel more time sensative. Luckily there was an attendant at the ticket machine and they walked me through getting our express tickets on the Keisei Skyliner, since I wanted to use our Welcome Suica cards for the base fair to activate them.

From there, I got too focused on matching the color (something I had read online) and went to the KeiSei Mainline platform (blue) when we instead needed to be on the Skyliner's (orange). After trying to reverse engineer where we should be standing for the car we needed to get on and feeling like something was off, we backtracked to the orange platform, and made it with minutes to spare. There was another attendant there helping manage the crowds of confused people, so it seems they put attendants in key areas during high traffic periods.

I was also focused on getting on the train that we had boarded before I was confident the eSim was working. The wifi on the train was glacially slow, but as people started to offload in nippoli it improved just enough to finish purchasing a 24 hour unlimited package (to test a little more before committing). Although while trying to test it, I accidentally downloaded a YouTube video and burned through the entire free trial data in one go. Bought 24 hours unlimited just to see if the thing was actually reliable.

## Tokyo: 15 Hours in Ueno

We got off the train a little tired and shuffled our way though tight, bustling streets toward the Airbnb. The crowds thinned pretty quickly the further we got into the neighborhood, one of those Tokyo things where the chaos has a hard edge and quiet lives just behind it. We fumbled around a little trying to piece together the clues left in the host's welcome message, and stepping inside felt like a small victory.

For dinner we picked a place nearby with pictures on the menu and enough empty tables that we didn't feel rushed. After, we hit the 7-Eleven to grab some cash and get some breakfast for the morning, as we heard there weren't many early morning breakfast options.

We woke up before most of the city did and walked to Sensō-ji, in part to avoid any crowds but also to stay on schedule. There were a handful of early tourists, the first we had seen since we left the airport, incense was already burning and only a couple of the vendors had just started setting up. It felt peaceful in a way it probably isn't by 10am. On the way back we walked through market streets as more vendors were starting to set up their shops, stopping at a bakery we smelled earlier in the morning.

On the way back to the station we took a detour and cut through Ueno Park to check out a shrine and well I just like parks. After that I got to use my first ticket machine without assistance and we navigated Ueno Station's Shinkansen signs to the right platform without a ton of trouble, although we were still a little scared from the previous experience.

## Nagano

We made it without issue and stashed our bags in a coin locker at Nagano Station so we could walk up towards the temple, Zenkoji, which turns out was a little uphill. It was a national holiday and we'd been counting on street food, but we hadn't gone far enough, so hungry and pivoting, we found a pork ramen place on a side market street with a line forming out front and decided that was a good enough sign.

The line barely moved and we were starving and impatient, discussing bailing, when they waved us in. You order by pressing buttons on a machine, novel to us but apparently fairly common, then sit at a small counter, seven seats total, while the two cooks prepared the food in front of us. The ramen was so good it reset the whole mood.

The temple after lunch was everything: the enormous statues, the pitch-black tunnel beneath the main hall where you shuffle forward in total darkness feeling for the wall, and then the deep resonant clang of the bell rolling out across the grounds. Worth the detour.

On the way back we stopped at the station grocery store to grab to-go dinners and breakfast supplies for Myoko-kogen. Walking aroung the store we noticed pretty much all the vegetables were comically oversized, attributed to the fertile soil and meticulous farming methods.

I didn't see a ticket machine for the local train (I could have missed it as my eye wasn't trained yet), but the ticket office for the local train was still open and paid cash (only option) for two tickets in what seemed like minutes before they closed up. We made the train without issue and enjoyed our first local train.

The Airbnb was directly across the street from where the train dropped us and it felt nice to just be able to make a short walk and not figure out transportation or directions. We ate our grocery store haul, got familiar with the AirBnB, and passed out.

We were up early again the next morning and walked around the town to get our bearings before everything opened. We circled back to the Welcome Center to confirm where the Snow Monkey bus picked up and get a feel for how much english they spoke. We confirmed the snow monkey shuttle pickup was at the bus station right across the other street from the Airbnb, which was good to know.

The bus ride was very beucolic, through farmlands backed by mountains with shades of blue. The monkey park trail is longer and steeper than it looks on paper and by that point had become a thick sheet of ice. On the way back down we found a vendor selling apple pies and yakimo, the roasted sweet potatoes that are everywhere in Japan in winter and somehow always exactly what you want. At some point I tested my earbuds' live translation feature and caught two young workers having an extended conversation about video games. It worked better than expected.

Dinner that night was a small adventure in reality vs online, two places were closed and one we'd have had to sit on the floor for and weren't up for it. So back to the supermarket for dinner and breakfast supplies, we cooked another good meal then sat by the pellet heater before passing out again. This seemed to be our rhythm in Myoko-kogen.

The next morning we caught a taxi to Landmark Onsen for what turned out to be a genuinely great first onsen experience — hot pools, the works. I'd had the onsen worker call a taxi for the return trip, which didn't go quite as smoothly as I'd pictured it going, but he got it sorted without complaint and didn't seem to think it was a strange ask.

From there we caught a train out to an aquarium in Joetsu, which was [worth it / a bit of a detour / whatever you thought]. Tried twice to get dinner at izakayas on the way back — turned away at both. Caught the train home, hit the supermarket one more time, detoured to the 7-Eleven for cash, cooked, fire, done.
The last morning we packed up and retraced the train route back through Nagano, then Omiya, before finally boarding the Hayabusa north toward Hokkaido.

The shinkansen from Ueno to Nagano took under two hours. We stowed our bags in coin lockers at the station and walked up Chuo-dori to Zenkoji Temple — a 1,400-year-old complex that felt like stepping into a different century. The pitch-black tunnel walk beneath the main hall is one of those experiences that's hard to describe. [*Try to describe it.*]

From Nagano, a shorter regional train dropped us in Myoko Kogen — a small ski town in Niigata Prefecture that sees serious snowfall and almost no Western tourists. Our Airbnb was a converted [*house/apartment/lodge — fill in what it actually was*], and the town felt immediately different from the resort towns we'd read about: quieter, more local, a supermarket stocked with miso soup packets and fresh onigiri.

**Onsen day** took us by bus to Akakura for a soak at Hotel Taiko, outdoor baths with unobstructed mountain views. [*What was the temperature outside? Was it snowing? Add the sensory detail here.*]

**Jigokudani** — the monkey park — was worth every step of the snowy forest trail. ## Hokkaido: Hakodate to Niseko

The Hayabusa shinkansen — Japan's fastest, running the undersea Seikan Tunnel into Hokkaido — departed from Omiya with the kind of silent punctuality that still surprises you every time. We picked up lunch at the station and watched Honshu give way to the straits and then to Hokkaido's wider, emptier landscape.

We stopped one night in Hakodate, a harbor city at Hokkaido's southern tip. The morning market near the station is the real reason to stop: fresh crab, sea urchin, salmon roe, and grilled scallops at 8am. [*What did you order? What was the best bite?*]

From Hakodate, a series of trains deposited us at Kutchan Station, gateway to Niseko. Our skis were waiting at the Hirafu Welcome Center — the luggage delivery had worked exactly as promised.

**Niseko** needs little introduction in powder circles, but the reality still exceeds the reputation. [*Opening powder day impressions — what were conditions like?*]

## Notes for the Next Time

- **Mail your gear early.** Yamato's ski bag delivery is the single best logistics decision you can make. Don't carry your skis on the train.
- **Get a Welcome Suica at Narita.** It works everywhere and saves you fumbling for change.
- **Myoko is underrated.** If you want quieter slopes and a more local feel, it's worth the detour.
- **Jigokudani in winter is the move.** Snow season is the reason to go.
- **Goshiki Onsen is worth the effort.** Plan the route, check the avy forecast, and go.
